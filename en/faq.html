Moroccan nationality: much more than a legal status

Moroccan nationality is not just a simple legal formality. It represents a deep connection between a person and Morocco, rooted in history, culture, and family memory. It's a way to officially recognize belonging that spans generations, even beyond borders or migrations.

Applying for or regaining Moroccan nationality, especially through descent, is often a strong act of identity. It allows individuals to reconnect with their origins, have their personal and family ties with the Kingdom recognized, and fully participate in the national community.

However, administrative procedures can be complex, especially when it comes to proving lineage with a Moroccan ancestor. That's why we created this FAQ: to guide you clearly and structurally through this process. It is primarily aimed at those whose parents or grandparents are of Moroccan origin and who wish to obtain nationality on this basis.

Our team combines precise legal expertise with a human understanding of individual journeys. We don't just process files: we accompany stories, re-establish connections, and help everyone embed their heritage in legal recognition.

We encourage you to undertake this journey of identity reconnection, relying on our rigorous, respectful, and deeply attentive support for the richness of personal paths.

General questions about Moroccan nationality

Moroccan nationality is primarily governed by the Moroccan Nationality Code. The main ways of acquiring it are:

  • By Descent (Filiation): Being born to a Moroccan father or a Moroccan mother. This is the most common path for the diaspora and our main area of expertise. (Note: For individuals born to a Moroccan mother before 2007, a specific declaration is often necessary.)
  • By Birth in Morocco (Jus Soli): In very specific cases (e.g., unknown parents, foreign parents themselves born in Morocco under certain conditions, young regular residents...).
  • By Marriage: For a foreigner married to a Moroccan national after 5 years of marriage and stable, continuous cohabitation in Morocco.
  • By Naturalization: A decision by the State granted to a foreigner meeting certain conditions (long-term residence, integration, etc.).

Each case is unique and requires individual analysis.

Yes, Morocco allows dual nationality. You generally do not have to renounce your other nationality to acquire Moroccan nationality.

It's important to know that when you are in Moroccan territory, the Moroccan authorities consider you primarily as Moroccan. This means Moroccan laws apply first, and consular assistance from your other country might be limited in certain situations.

The specific documents always depend on your personal situation and the basis on which you are applying for nationality (paternal lineage, maternal lineage, date of birth, etc.).

Typically, you will need to provide recent and complete civil status records (yours, those of your Moroccan parents/grandparents) to establish family ties irrefutably.

The crucial proof concerns the Moroccan nationality of your ascendants at the time of the relevant transmission (your birth, your parent's birth...). This may include a copy of their Electronic National Identity Card (CNIE), an old certificate of nationality, or other official and conclusive Moroccan documents.

Proving the direct line of descent can be complex, especially over several generations, and often involves in-depth research in civil registries (Adouls, civil registrars) or Moroccan consular archives.

Remember that documents issued by foreign authorities generally need to be translated into Arabic by a translator sworn before the Moroccan courts. They often also require legalization or an apostille (depending on international conventions) to be recognized in Morocco.

Ezra Maroc helps you identify the exact list of documents needed for your specific case and assists you in the collection, translation, and legalization/apostille processes.

No, knowledge of the language (dialectal Arabic, classical Arabic, or Amazigh) is not a legal requirement for obtaining Moroccan nationality by descent (jus sanguinis).

This language requirement mainly concerns the naturalization process, where integration into Moroccan society is assessed.

Procedures and Timelines

The general procedure involves meticulously compiling a file containing all required proofs (civil status, proof of ancestor's nationality, proof of lineage).

This file is then submitted to the competent authority: most often the Court of First Instance (nationality department) in Morocco, or sometimes via the Moroccan Consulate in your place of residence.

An investigation phase follows, during which the documents are verified by the King's Prosecutor and the judge in charge of nationality. Further investigations may be requested.

The procedure concludes with an official decision: either the direct issuance of a Moroccan Certificate of Nationality (CNM) or a declarative judgment of nationality rendered by the court.

We guide you through each step, from evidence gathering to submission and follow-up, to ensure compliance and maximize the chances of success for your application.

Timelines vary considerably depending on the complexity of the file, the workload of the relevant administrations (courts, consulates), and the need for possible investigations or verifications. There is no legal or regulatory deadline guaranteed by Moroccan law for deciding on a nationality application.

Based purely on our experience and for indicative purposes only:

  • Descent (simple, well-documented cases): A timeframe of 6 months to 2 years is sometimes observed, but this range remains optimistic and not guaranteed.
  • Descent (complex cases): Files requiring extensive research (old archives, indirect evidence), involving multiple generations, or presenting administrative complications (missing documents, transcriptions) can easily exceed 3 years, or even more.
  • Marriage / Naturalization: These processes are generally longer and strictly depend on meeting the legal requirements (duration of effective residence, etc.).

Our role is to prepare the most complete and well-argued file possible to try to optimize these timelines, and to ensure regular follow-up with the authorities, but the administration remains sovereign regarding its processing speed.

Yes, tracking is possible, although it requires method and perseverance. It is essential to carefully keep a complete copy of your submitted file as well as the official deposit receipt, which usually contains a reference number.

Regular follow-up with the competent administration (court clerk's office, consular service) is recommended to inquire about the progress. However, be aware that responses can be brief and processing times sometimes long.

In case of an explicit refusal decision (or sometimes implicit after a long silence), do not be discouraged. Avenues for appeal exist (appeal, cassation appeal depending on the case) to challenge the decision before higher courts.

Our legal team is experienced in these litigation procedures and can advise you on the appropriateness of an appeal, and assist you in its preparation and submission if necessary.

The need to physically travel to Morocco depends on your geographical location, the consulate you fall under, and the exact nature of your procedure. It is not always systematic but remains common.

For identity documents (CNIE, passport): Once nationality is obtained and proven (by CNM or transcribed judgment), a trip is often required for the application and collection of these documents. However, some large consulates (e.g., Paris, Madrid, Montreal, New York, etc.) are now equipped to capture biometric data. Check with your consulate to see if they offer this service.

For the judicial procedure (if applicable): If your case requires an appearance before a judge (e.g., for a declarative judgment of nationality), certain steps or hearings can sometimes be delegated by the Moroccan judge to the consulate via a "letter rogatory". However, this depends on the judge's decision and the nature of the act to be performed. A personal appearance in Morocco may therefore still be necessary in some judicial cases.

Conclusion: Physical presence may be required at different stages. A personalized assessment of your file by us will help better anticipate any necessary trips in your specific case.

Special Cases

Absolutely. Moroccan citizens of the Jewish faith and their descendants can claim Moroccan nationality by descent, under the same conditions as any other Moroccan, in accordance with the Nationality Code. The Moroccan Constitution of 2011 explicitly recognizes the Hebraic tributary as one of the components of the united national identity.

The main challenge, as with many applications by descent concerning families who emigrated long ago, often lies in gathering documentary evidence. It must be possible to irrefutably attest to the direct line of descent from the Moroccan ancestor (father or mother depending on the case and era) and the retention of their Moroccan nationality (that they did not lose or voluntarily renounce it before the transmission).

Searching for old documents (Hebrew or civil status records, travel documents, consular registers, proof of residence...) can be complex. Ezra Maroc has developed specific expertise in this area, including research in various archives and the rigorous compilation of these files to meet legal requirements.

For a minor child, acquiring Moroccan nationality mainly depends on the parents' nationality at the time of birth and the legislation in force at that date.

According to the current Nationality Code (since the 2007 reform), a child born to a Moroccan father OR a Moroccan mother is Moroccan. Transmission through the mother is therefore equivalent to that through the father. Acquisition is generally automatic by right but requires registration in the Moroccan civil registry to be effective.

Note: For children born to a Moroccan mother and a foreign father before the 2007 reform, nationality was not automatic. Specific procedures for declaration or application by the mother or the child upon reaching majority exist to recognize this nationality (Article 6 of the Code).

It is important to note that Kafala (legal guardianship in Moroccan and Islamic law, which is not a plenary adoption severing original lineage) does not automatically confer Moroccan nationality on the fostered child (makfoul). Specific residency conditions and a distinct procedure are provided for the possible naturalization of the child under Kafala.

Loss and Reintegration

Involuntary loss of Moroccan nationality is very rare and strictly regulated by law. It can only occur in specific cases provided for by the Nationality Code (e.g., voluntary acquisition of a foreign nationality with an express request to lose Moroccan nationality before certain dates, holding public office for a foreign state without authorization, serious acts against state security...).

It is possible to voluntarily request to renounce Moroccan nationality (act of repudiation), for example, if another nationality requires it. However, this renunciation is never automatic. It requires express authorization granted by decree from the Moroccan government, which examines the request on a case-by-case basis and may refuse it.

For individuals who may have lost Moroccan nationality in the past (e.g., due to old laws on marriage or residence abroad), procedures for reintegration into Moroccan nationality are provided for by the Code, subject to meeting certain conditions (notably establishing habitual residence in Morocco).
